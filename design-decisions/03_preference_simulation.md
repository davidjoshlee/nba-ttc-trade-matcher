# Design Decision: Preference Simulation

## Decision
Team preferences are automatically generated by analyzing roster composition against an "ideal roster template" and ranking available players by how well they fill identified gaps.

## How it works
1. **Gap analysis**: Compare each team's role distribution to an ideal (e.g., need ≥1 Playmaker, ≥2 Floor Spacers). Roles filled by secondary-role players count at 0.5x.
2. **Urgency scoring**: (ideal_count - actual_count) / ideal_count. Score of 1.0 = total absence of a needed role.
3. **Player ranking**: For each available player on other teams, preference_score = role_urgency × player_quality_score.
4. **Quality score**: Weighted composite of pts, reb, ast, stl, blk, minus turnovers.

## Tradeoff: Realism vs. Tractability
- **What we gain**: Fully automated preferences for all 30 teams. No human input needed to run the demo. Preferences are data-driven and consistent.
- **What we lose**: Real GMs consider chemistry, age, injury history, contract length, scheme fit, cap flexibility — none of which we model. All teams use the same "ideal composition" template, but in reality team strategies differ (some want to play small-ball, others want traditional bigs).

## Why not human input?
The proposal mentions managers providing their own preferences. For the prototype, simulated preferences let us demo the full system without needing 30 human inputs. The architecture supports swapping in human preferences — the TTC algorithm only needs a ranked list per team.

## Baseline urgency for Rotation Players
Players classified as "Rotation Player" (no strong archetype) receive a small baseline urgency (0.1) so they can still participate in trades. Without this, the majority of available players would never appear in any team's preferences.
